Posted: February 28, 2020
This game is a great time burner.
For years I waited for a game reminicent of Downhill Domination, this has filled that gap nicely.
I played it for free on Xbox Game Pass, then bought it for Steam so that I could play with mods.
Then I spent half my time on the game cruising through the two career paths and completing the sponsorship challenges.
And the other half Is spent just fooling around in the main lobby park. There you can watch others pull off tricks that you can learn or practice wheelies and nose manuals to the point of perfection. Just put some spotify on (or listen to the great built in soundtrack) and just practice. It's a great escape.
I remember being in the lobby and I saw someone just cruise by in a stable wheelie and just thought "huh, I wanna learn that" so over about a week, i perfected wheelie control.
Then saw someone do a 360 Backflip and thought, "I wounder if I could do that into a vert ramp" so I did.
Pulled off a nice 360 can-can? Now add a little no-hander or tweak at the end. Yeh nice.
Then I learned what jumps not to go for, which is half the challenge, you wouldn't go full steam at everything at a real bike park would you? You would die. And you will on this too.
This game is about finesse, make it to the bottom and clean land every trick, know what not to do on certain take-off ramps and learn how to recover when you look like you're going to mess it up. This is what it is all about.
I would say that once you have mastered all of the available trick combinations, it can get a bit same same, But then its up to you to learn how to turn those tricks into runs that deliver flow and finesse. Again, that's the real art of this game.
What is missing, and probably for the best, is chat. The only form of communication you have with others is to ding your bell or hoot your horn, or bunnyhop wildly in front of them to try to convey some kind of message.
But i do feel, that lack of chat is what keeps this game rage-free and troll-free. Although it would be good to compliment other players during runs to build the hype.
Overall, a fantastic game and with the mods and an active/talented mod community, this game will last a long time for me.

Posted: October 12, 2019
Descenders is an amazing Downhill Biking game, You can go fast or go for the tricks, Customize your biker, Explore open levels or go for the random generated tracks, It is the ultimate Downhill game.
The game start with a nice tutorial where it explains the basics of riding the bike, The controls are done very nicely. From there you get into the main menu, Which is a small BMX park where you can see other players messing around, When you bike off the map you will explode and get launched back to the center, Looks very familliar :P
In the main menu/lobby there is a shed where you can customize your bike and rider, There are a ton of clothing parts, helmets, bikes, accessories, etc that you are able to unlock. You start with practically nothing, But you will unlock something after every ride, depending on your performance you can get rare stuff or more common stuff.
When you are done messing around at the park you can start anything by pressing the start button, Here you can launch The Career, Career Plus, Freeride or mods. Career is a mode where you have limited lifes, You lose 1 or 2 lifes if you crash, But you can gain lifes by completing the optional objective for every level.
It is the goal to find the exit level in each Biome, Each biome has a bunch of dots that all represent a level, You start at the beginning and every time you complete a level you can play the dots that are next to the completed dot. There are many different kinds of levels, You can complete them all, but the goal is to just find unlock and complete the exit level
The Exit level is usually an insane jump, so saving up some lifes might be usefull, You also gain perks that are active for the duration of your run, After you lose your last life you have to start over drom the beginning. You can start from selected biomes if you completed their main jump a few times, But you really miss out on the easy extra lifes in the earlier biomes.
Career Plus is the same as Career only it has 4 different, more challenging Biomes. Freeride is like a custom Career where you can modify the randomness of the run. Make it easier, make it harder, Whatever floats your boat. Then there are the mods, Which is just insane, They can be the same downhill challenges, Free Roam maps, Trial Bike Challenges, You name it.
Workshop support is brand new, But there are already a few nice maps, Like BikeOut, Which is WipeOut with a bike, It has checkpoints, Then there are like 5 insane skate parks and some other stuff, I am curious towards the insane maps people will build using this powerfull workshop support. I think we will see a lot of Trial bike Challenges.
The Entire game is online, Everywhere you look you see other players, How this works with the Random generation beats me, But I suppose they thought of something clever. If you don't like the idea of everyone seeing you crash over and over again you can choose to disable the online functionality.
The game also has a great soundtrack, And just stunning graphics, I think this is the best downhill game ever made. It could however use a better trick system, The LB + RJ is great, but there are only a few tricks that you can do with it. I would love to see more insane tricks that you could do for those biggest airtimes.
Overall the game is just amazing, I don't see anyone not liking this game. I have to give this game a solid 10, It truly has everything you could ask for in a biking game.

Posted: May 7, 2019
Buy this if you are interested in the idea of riding a bike down quick and short generated levels, risking your HP to do dangerous tricks and rack up score. Although the individual levels feel similar, if you beat the boss jump at the end you'll travel through up to 8 largely different biomes, unlocking shortcuts along the way (Spelunky style) to jump straight to the later biomes.
Some people buy this expecting an open world game or a game where you'll have to moderate your stamina and thirst, or changing the thickness of your tires and tinkering with the suspension of your bike; this game isn't that kind of simulation. Instead, Descenders is a really enjoyable arcade roguelite.

Ok, where do I start, for starters I'm an actual downhill rider, and I was longing for a decent and playable mountain biking game. I randomly looked again on steam and stumbled upon Descenders, instantly bought the pre-access after seeing the trailer video.
Let's start with the game
- Very fun gameplay mechanics, a little hard to begin with but gets better with training, the learning curve was actually not frustrating at all and the controls have a very good potential and are responsive and well balanced.
- Physics are good. For an game under development, this is really good actually. The feeling of speed is spot on, and the tricks are fun to land and feel satisfying.
- The freaking helmet cam. Let's talk about that. It's insane and I want it as an option to switch during my runs as I please! It's insane, the feeling of not seeing how you're gonna land a trick, and the feeling of having to lean back during a backflip is just SPOT. ON. I can't recommend this game enough to mountain bikers who like downhill riding and throwing tricks/slopestyle riding. Thank you for this very well made game. Can't wait for more development and updates.

Posted: February 24
If you're at the point of reading reviews to try to decide whether or not to buy this game, I'm assuming you find yourself in the same dilemma I found myself in: Should I buy Descenders or Rider's Republic? I wanted a downhill biking game and it was such a hard decision. Well, luckily I own both, so I can compare them side by side so you don't have to. I'll just copy+paste my review on both so you can compare the two without purchasing both. (Or, heck, DO buy them both. They're both EXCELLENT games in their own regard, with each of them having unique things that make them 'stand out' from the other.)
So without further ado, Descenders vs Rider's Republic.
Q. Which one 'feels' more like biking? Which feels more natural?
A. Hands down, Descenders. I don't know what it is, but I've never played a biking game where the riding just felt absolutely natural and 'right.' Don't get me wrong, the riding in RR is still great, but there's something about the flow of D that makes it feel like you're actually riding a bike. (Also, RR, despite being loads of fun, can feel a little glitchy at times.. and occasionally, if my Steam Deck lags a little, my rider will come to a dead stop in a heartbeat... which is somewhat problematic in a game all about maintaining high speeds.) In D you will feel the trails, feel the speed, feel the jumps. It feels right.
Q. Which has more to do in the game?
A. Hands down RR. I'll speak a little more about D in a bit, but let's be real here: RR has bikes, skis/snowboards, wing-suits and more (and skating if you buy the DLC's). There are all kinds of side quests in RR, different types of races, all kinds of goals and achievements... It is an open-world setting, and that world is filled with things to do. In contrast, while the levels in Descenders are large, the only kind of 'open world,' so to speak, are the lobbies and the multiplayer arenas (where you can't gain Rep).
That's not to say there's nothing to do in D. You can mess around in the lobby with other players, you can go on adventurous rides, conquer boss jumps, and pull off great stunts. You can go play in the multiplayer worlds. There's plenty to do - it's just not filled to the brim with content like RR.
However - it needs to be said that sometimes quality vs quantity is a thing. Maybe more on that next.
Q. Which has more VARIETY?
A. That's right - although RR has a TON of stuff to do, it HAS to be pointed out that Descenders' levels are procedurally-generated, so each time you go on a ride your paths will be different. Now, it's not so different that you'll feel like you're in a different world every time.. but the paths ARE different enough that it will absolutely keep you on your toes. You can't attempt to 'memorize' the paths like you can with RR. You'll ABSOLUTELY have to establish some real flow in D.
Q. Which has better graphics?
A. This one is VERY hard. While RR is an enormous game filled to the brim with details, there's something extremely beautiful about D as well. Both of them have highly detailed worlds, and each one has their own 'feel' to them. Descenders makes you feel like you're in this lonely, far-away natural world, while Rider's Republic makes you feel like you've gone on vacation to a huge park of sorts filled to the brim with people. The 'world' seems to flow a little more naturally in D, while the cuts between biomes in RR can sometimes feel a little abrupt. I don't know if there's a clear winner on graphics - despite RR having way more assets, the simple beauty of D has its own stunning appeal. I think this one will just be a matter of opinion for each player.
Also has to be said that it can take a bit of a beast to run RR at full settings. My Steam Deck seems to be able to handle most graphics-intensive games fairly well, but I actually had to turn down the settings on RR just a little because I was losing FPS and it was getting glitchy.
Q. Which has more multi-player/better function?
A. Probably RR, although the multi-player in D still adds a lot of fun. In D you can ride around with others in the lobby, go to multiplayer worlds full of random stuff, and encounter other players out on the trails. Far as I'm aware, that's about it. Out on the trails you're not really racing others, but it's still cool to encounter them.
Meanwhile, in RR, there are 'mass races' with 50+ other players (although I'm pretty sure there are plenty of players who are able to cheat), you can challenge others, etc. So yes, there's a lot more competition in RR. It's also a little more seamless in RR - in D, players have a tendency to 'glitch' and fly all over the place. Both are fun, but if multi-player competition is extremely important to you, probably RR.
Q. Which has more 'stuff' to get?
A. No clue. I have LOADS AND LOADS of 'stuff' in D. Bikes, accessories, shirts, pants, helmets, it seems like there is just no end of customization stuff to get in D. Meanwhile, in RR, I have plenty of 'stuff' like bikes, skis, snowboards, wingsuits, and all kinds of garbage... but very little in the way of clothes and accessories. Crappily, it seems that if you want clothes or things like that, you've got to actually purchase (like, with real money) in-game currency to spend. I have no desire to spend money on clothes. That stuff should be won by playing, just like in D. Seems greedy of RR.
One difference is that in D, the different 'bikes' are mainly just skins, whereas the different gear in RR actually have their own performance stats. So this is again a matter of opinion - if you like things to be fair and be more about skill, you'd prefer D, where everyone's bikes are on a more equal playing field... if you want to have the 'fastest gear around,' then I guess RR.
But remember, again, there are TONS of things to be had in D, and a lot of stuff in RR that is locked behind a paywall. It will be up to you to decide how you want to play it.
Q. Better music/sounds?
A. No clear winner. I don't usually like in-game music so I often turn music in games off, but in both of these games I leave it on. The music in D gives you more of that 'I'm riding with determination in some far-off and lonely world,' while the music in RR tends to give more of that 'woo-hoo, I'm riding like crazy in this adrenaline-junky world filled with people.' Each on has their own appeal.
For the sound effects, I prefer the ones in D. Once again, they just feel more natural. (Despite being a somewhat smaller production, there's actually a lot about D that just feels more 'natural.')
Q. Which has a better 'reputation' system?
A. IDK. Each one has a 'reputation' system where you can show off your skill in the game... It's REP in D, and 'stars' in RR. I'll say that the rep of other players is a lot more visible in RR. You of course earn rep and stars by doing well in the game.
Q. Which one has more daily/seasonal events?
A. RR. D has a 'daily run' which can be a lot of fun, but I hear that a lot of folks are able to cheat and get billions of points or whatever. (But then again, from some of the races I've done on RR, I am almost certain there's cheating there as well.) Meanwhile, there are always a ton of on-going events in RR.
Q. Is there a clear winner between the two?
A. Not for me. I play both. When I feel like playing with a ton of variety or against other players, I'll switch over to RR. When I want an experience that feels just about as natural as it can get, I switch over to D. When I want to try to earn the fastest/best gear, I'll play RR... when I want to win an enormous variety of customization, I'll play D. When I want a more open world 'free play' style I'll play RR.. when I just want to go through procedurally generated series of levels I'll play D. I really do believe that each game has its own unique strengths and weaknesses.
Q. Worth full price?
A. Wait for a sale if you can. But for either one, they're both fantastic, so it wouldn't be the end of the world to buy at full price.
